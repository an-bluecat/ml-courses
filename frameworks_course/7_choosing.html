The deep pattern: PyTorch is wedded to the CUDA ecosystem, JAX to the XLA/TPU ecosystem — and each is courting the other's hardware through the compiler layer (OpenXLA/StableHLO as the shared IR). Your hardware often chooses your framework before you do: TPU shop → JAX; CUDA cluster → PyTorch; a MacBook → PyTorch (MPS) or NumPy.

Kernels, when the library isn't enough

Custom-kernel escape hatches mirror the alliance: torch → Triton (write Python-ish tiles, get GPU kernels — also what Inductor generates); jax → Pallas (same idea, lowers to Triton on GPU and Mosaic on TPU). Both exist because the era of hand-written CUDA C++ for every idea (FlashAttention's origin story) didn't scale to the research population.

The historical irony: TF's "graph as artifact" thesis won completely — every framework now exports a compiled, Python-free artifact — just as TF itself stopped being the place you'd build the model.

Weights became the real interchange format

Note what actually travels between systems in 2026: not graphs — weights (safetensors files) plus architecture code reimplemented per framework. Llama weights train in PyTorch, serve in vLLM, quantize into LiteRT, get ported to MaxText/JAX. The model IS the checkpoint; the program is rewritten per venue. This is why Chapter 6's "state as data" perspective matters beyond JAX.

What should you learn, in order (2026)?

  • NumPy deeply — it's the grammar of the other three (you're most of the way there after Chapter 1).
  • PyTorch as your daily driver — every job posting, paper and library assumes it.
  • JAX as your second language — it teaches the functional/compiler mental model that PyTorch itself is drifting toward (torch.func, compile), and it's the ticket to TPU-scale work. Part 3 finishes this.
  • TF as reading knowledge — enough to maintain legacy systems and export to LiteRT; don't start new projects in it without a reason.
