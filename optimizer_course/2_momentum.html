Momentum — give the step a memory

Chapter 2. One extra number per parameter turns "react to this gradient" into "trust the trend" — with a physics you can feel and a failure mode you must know.

One new idea: don't step on the gradient — step on a running total

The update (exactly as torch implements it)

buf ← μ·buf + grad # the memory: decay the old total, add today's gradient w ← w − lr·buf # step on the TOTAL, not on today's gradient

Two changes from SGD: the optimizer now carries state — one number buf per parameter, surviving between steps — and the step uses that state instead of the raw gradient. μ ("momentum", usually 0.9) controls how long the memory lasts: each step the old contents keep 90% of their value.

The name is physics: think of buf as velocity. The gradient is a force pushing a ball; μ<1 is friction. SGD is a ball on flypaper — each push moves it once and it stops. Momentum is a ball on ice with slight drag — pushes accumulate.

Feel the accumulation: push with force 1, every step

output — real run: buf = 0.9·buf + 1.0 t= 1: buffer = 1.000 t= 2: buffer = 1.900 t= 3: buffer = 2.710 t= 5: buffer = 4.095 t=10: buffer = 6.513 limit: 1/(1-0.9) = 10.0 -> momentum multiplies terminal speed ~10x

A gradient that keeps pointing the same way ends up moving the weight ten times as fast as SGD would (with μ=0.9; the general limit is 1/(1−μ)). Nothing converged yet — this is just arithmetic of the buffer — but you can already guess who benefits: the gentle, consistent direction from Chapter 1's bowl, the one SGD starved.

Refresher: why does the buffer approach exactly 10?

Unroll it: after many steps of constant gradient 1, the buffer holds 1 + 0.9 + 0.9² + 0.9³ + … — today's push, plus 90% of yesterday's, plus 81% of the day before's. That's a geometric series, and it sums to 1/(1−0.9) = 10. Same idea in reverse gives the memory's horizon: contributions older than ~1/(1−μ) ≈ 10 steps have decayed to near nothing, so "μ = 0.9" reads as "average over roughly the last 10 gradients." Keep that reading — Adam's β₁=0.9 and β₂=0.999 (last ~10, last ~1000) will reuse it.

Effect 2: where gradients alternate, the buffer averages them away

The buffer arithmetic, side by side — trace every number yourself

Chapter 1's zigzag was w2's gradient flipping sign every step. Feed the buffer two diets — steady +1 versus alternating +1, −1, +1, … — and do the arithmetic (buf = 0.9·buf + g):

tsteady g = +1alternating g = ±1
11.0000.9·0 + 1 = 1.000
21.9000.9·1.000 − 1 = −0.100
32.7100.9·(−0.100) + 1 = 0.910
43.4390.9·0.910 − 1 = −0.181
54.0950.9·(−0.181) + 1 = 0.837
→ ∞→ 10.0bounded near ±0.5

Same μ, same formula, opposite outcomes: agreement compounds toward 10×; disagreement cancels toward ~0.5×. Momentum is a filter — it passes the trend and mutes the flip-flop. The zigzag direction gets smaller effective steps exactly where SGD was wasting motion, and the valley-floor direction gets bigger ones. One buffer does both jobs, automatically, per parameter.

Where this matters most in real training: mini-batch noise

In real training you don't even need a canyon to get flip-flopping gradients — every mini-batch disagrees with the last one, because each is a different random sample of data. The batch-to-batch noise alternates in sign (it averages to zero over time); the true underlying descent direction repeats. The filter you just traced treats them accordingly: noise cancels in the buffer, signal accumulates. This is why momentum helps even on landscapes with no pathological curvature at all, and why μ=0.9 is on by default in nearly every SGD recipe in the wild.

The honest page: momentum is not free speed

Crank both knobs and watch the ball leave the valley — real run

Chapter 1 used lr=0.09 for SGD: 24 steps to converge. Surely adding momentum makes it faster? No — it triples the time. Watch the trajectory:

A pendulum. steps to loss<0.01: SGD(lr .09): 24 | momentum(lr .09, μ .9): 69

Both paths real. Faint red: chapter 1's SGD zigzag (lr .09). Orange: the same lr with μ=0.9 — the ball shoots straight through the minimum, climbs the far wall to w1 ≈ −7, and swings back. Physics kept its promise: momentum conserves motion, including motion you wanted to stop.

So which is it — 6× faster or 3× slower? The full grid, measured

output — real run: steps to loss < 0.01 on the bowl lr=0.005: SGD 459 | mom μ=.9 85 | mom μ=.5 225 lr= 0.01: SGD 228 | mom μ=.9 37 | mom μ=.5 109 lr= 0.02: SGD 113 | mom μ=.9 40 | mom μ=.5 51 lr= 0.05: SGD 44 | mom μ=.9 73 | mom μ=.5 15 lr= 0.09: SGD 24 | mom μ=.9 69 | mom μ=.5 13 lr= 0.11: SGD diverges | mom μ=.9 55 | mom μ=.5 11

Read it like a map, not a verdict:

  • Small lr → big μ wins huge (459→85, 228→37): steps are timid, so the ball's 10× buildup is pure profit.
  • Big lr → big μ overshoots (24→69): steps were already bold; 10× bold is a pendulum.
  • μ=0.5 at big lr is the sweet spot here (13, 11 steps — the best numbers on the board): a 2× boost (1/(1−0.5)) on top of near-maximal lr.
  • And the quietly profound row: at lr=0.11, SGD diverges but momentum converges — the friction of averaging lets you survive past SGD's cliff. Momentum doesn't just move faster along the valley; it widens the range of usable learning rates.

lr and μ are one control system, not two knobs — a useful rule of thumb hiding in the table: what matters most is roughly lr/(1−μ), the terminal step size. 0.01 with μ=0.9 "feels like" 0.1; that's why its column resembles SGD at ten times the lr.

Refresher: Nesterov momentum — the checkbox next to μ

SGD(momentum=0.9, nesterov=True) changes one thing: the gradient is evaluated as if you had already taken the momentum part of the step — "look where the ball is about to be, not where it is." Peeking ahead means the ball starts braking one step earlier when it approaches a wall, which softens exactly the overshoot this page demonstrated. Same buffer, same cost, usually a small win; common in convnet recipes. If μ is a ball, Nesterov is a ball with eyes.

The fine print in torch — and why it bites paper-reproducers

Two "momentums" circulate in the wild

# torch (what you've traced all chapter): buf ← μ·buf + grad # raw sum — reaches grad·1/(1−μ) ≈ 10×grad # many papers (EMA form): buf ← μ·buf + (1−μ)·grad # weighted average — reaches ≈ 1×grad

Identical behavior — except the second is 10× smaller (with μ=0.9), which means the effective step differs by 10× at the same lr. Port a paper's lr into torch without checking which form it used, and your training runs 10× hotter or colder than intended. This single formula difference is one of the most common silent reproduction bugs in the field. When in doubt: torch's SGD uses the raw-sum form above (its dampening arg can add the (1−μ), default 0).

Carry forward

  • Momentum = one remembered number per parameter: buf = μ·buf + g, step on buf.
  • Agreement compounds (→ 1/(1−μ)×), disagreement cancels — one filter fixes both the starved direction and the zigzag/noise.
  • It's a ball: it overshoots when lr·μ is too bold, and it widens the stable-lr range. Tune lr and μ as one unit (think lr/(1−μ)).
  • Still unsolved: w1 and w2 get the same lr. Momentum reshapes steps over time, not per parameter. That second cure is next.
