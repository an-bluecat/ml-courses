Per-parameter scale — AdaGrad → RMSProp

Chapter 3. Momentum reshaped steps over time. This chapter reshapes them per parameter: every weight gets its own effective learning rate, set by its own history.

The second disease, at its real scale

Two parameters, gradients 1000× apart

Forget curvature for a moment — here's the version of the problem you meet in every real network. One parameter sits in a hot path (say, a LayerNorm gain touched by every token): gradient −24. Another sits in a cold path (an embedding row for a rare word): gradient −0.024. Same model, same loss, same lr=0.1. What SGD does:

output — real run g= -24.000: SGD lr=0.1 step = 2.4000 g= -0.024: SGD lr=0.1 step = 0.0024

The hot parameter takes a wild 2.4-unit leap (remember chapter 1: that's deep in overshoot territory). The cold one moves by 0.0024 — it would need a thousand steps to move what the hot one moved in one. One lr cannot serve both. Lower it and the cold parameter freezes entirely; raise it and the hot one explodes. This is chapter 1's bowl again, but now with a spread of scales no hand-tuning can bridge.

State the wish precisely

What we want: every parameter should move about lr per step — regardless of how big its gradient happens to be. The gradient should choose the direction; the learning rate should choose the distance; the gradient's magnitude should stop tyrannizing the step size.

The move that achieves it: divide each parameter's step by (something like) the typical size of its own gradient. Big-gradient parameters get divided by a big number, small-gradient parameters by a small one — both land near lr. The chapter is about what "typical size" should mean.

AdaGrad: remember every gradient ever — and pay for it

The update (2011)

G ← G + grad² # per-parameter running SUM of squared gradients w ← w − lr · grad / (√G + ε) # divide by the root of the history

√G is the "typical gradient size so far" (root of the accumulated square). A parameter that has seen gradients of ~24 has √G ≈ 24·√t; one that's seen ~0.024 has √G ≈ 0.024·√t — each gets divided by its own scale, and the wish from page 0 comes true. AdaGrad was built for exactly the rare-word-embedding case, and it works.

The fatal flaw: the sum only grows

G adds a positive number every step and never forgets. So √G grows like √t forever, and the effective step shrinks like 1/√t — even if you're nowhere near done. Watch it with a constant gradient (nothing is converging; the landscape isn't changing; the optimizer just… ages):

output — real run: constant g = −24, lr = 0.1 step 1: effective step = 0.1000 step 2: effective step = 0.0707 step 10: effective step = 0.0316 step 100: effective step = 0.0100

By step 100 the optimizer moves at a tenth of its starting speed, purely because time passed. For convex problems that's even provably the right schedule; for deep learning — where you may need full-speed steps at epoch 50 when the loss landscape shifts — it's a slow death. The idea (divide by gradient scale) is right; the memory (infinite) is wrong.

RMSProp: same idea, sliding-window memory

One-symbol fix: replace the sum with a decaying average

v ← β·v + (1−β)·grad² # EMA of squared gradients (β = 0.9: last ~10 steps) w ← w − lr · grad / (√v + ε) # divide by the RECENT typical size

This is chapter 2's memory trick applied to the denominator: v is an exponential moving average (the (1−β) makes it a true average — it settles at the typical g², not 10× it). Old gradients fade instead of accumulating, so the effective lr tracks the recent gradient scale and never dies of old age. The name says exactly what it divides by: Root Mean Square, Propagated over time.

The wish, granted — real numbers

output — real run: first step, lr = 0.1, β = 0.9 g= -24.000: SGD step = 2.4000 | RMSProp step = 0.3162 g= -0.024: SGD step = 0.0024 | RMSProp step = 0.3162

Look at the right column: identical steps for gradients 1000× apart. Trace the big one by hand: v = 0.9·0 + 0.1·(−24)² = 57.6; √v = 7.589; step = 0.1·24/7.589 = 0.3162. The tiny one: v = 0.1·(0.024)² → √v = 0.00759 → same 0.3162. Each parameter is divided by its own recent scale, so the scale cancels — direction survives, magnitude is normalized. Page 0's wish, delivered.

But squint at that number: 0.3162 ≠ lr = 0.1

The wish said "move about lr." We got 3.16× lr. Why: on the very first step, v = 0.1·g² — the EMA has only collected 10% of one sample, so it underestimates the typical g² by 10×, and √v underestimates by √10 ≈ 3.16 — inflating the step by exactly that. A freshly-initialized EMA lies small; dividing by a lie-small number means stepping too big, right at the start of training when weights are random and caution matters most.

Remember 0.3162. Fixing precisely this number — by explicitly correcting the young EMA's underestimate — is one of the two things Adam adds in the next chapter.

Refresher: why divide by the SQUARE ROOT of v, not v itself?

Units. v averages g², so its natural scale is (gradient)² — for g ≈ 24, v ≈ 576. Divide g by v and you'd get 24/576 = 1/24: the step would shrink for big gradients and blow up for tiny ones far too aggressively (1000× spread becomes 1000× the other way). Take the root first and √v ≈ |g|, so g/√v ≈ ±1 — a clean, dimensionless "which way, in units of my own typical size." Then lr alone sets the distance. Rule of thumb: anything that averages squares must be un-squared before it meets the gradient.

Refresher: what is ε actually for, and why 1e-8?

Pure crash insurance: a parameter whose gradients have all been ~0 has v ≈ 0, and g/√v would be 0/0 or a huge number from float dust. Adding ε ≈ 1e-8 to the denominator caps the amplification at about 10⁸ — irrelevant whenever gradients are healthy (√v ≫ ε), decisive when they're not. It's a tunable in torch (eps=) and occasionally matters for real: mixed-precision training sometimes raises it (e.g. 1e-6) because float16 gradient noise makes tiny-v estimates untrustworthy. If you ever see a loss spike traced to one strange parameter group, checking ε is a legitimate move, not superstition.

Two memories now exist — put them on one map

Numerator memory vs denominator memory

Momentum (ch. 2)RMSProp (this chapter)
remembersthe gradients themselves (with sign)the gradients' squares (size only)
lives in the update asthe numerator — what direction, how hardthe denominator — the per-parameter yardstick
fixeszigzag & noise over time; starved slow directionsscale spread across parameters

They are not rivals — they answer different questions ("which way is the trend?" vs "how big is a normal step for this particular weight?") and they compose naturally: keep an EMA of gradients for the numerator, an EMA of squared gradients for the denominator, divide. That composition — plus one honesty patch for young EMAs (your 0.3162) — has a name, and it's the default optimizer of the deep-learning era.

Carry forward

  • The wish: every parameter moves ~lr; its gradient picks direction, not distance.
  • AdaGrad grants it by dividing by √(sum of g²) — but the infinite memory makes lr decay to zero on a schedule set by time, not progress.
  • RMSProp swaps in an EMA: divide by the recent RMS gradient. Equalizes 1000× scale spreads (real: both steps 0.3162).
  • The loose thread: a young EMA underestimates → first steps are ~3.16× too big. Carry the number 0.3162 into the next chapter.
