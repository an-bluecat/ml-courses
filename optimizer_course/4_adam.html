The name is literal: Adaptive moment estimation ‚ÄĒ m and v are the first and second "moments" (statistics-speak for mean and mean-of-squares) of the gradient, estimated by EMAs.

Refresher: why ő≤‚āĀ = 0.9 but ő≤‚āā = 0.999? Why remember scale longer than trend?

Chapter 2's horizon rule: an EMA with decay ő≤ averages roughly the last 1/(1‚ąíő≤) samples. So ő≤‚āĀ=0.9 ‚Üí the trend is "the last ~10 gradients," and ő≤‚āā=0.999 ‚Üí the scale is "the last ~1000." The asymmetry is deliberate: a gradient's direction can legitimately flip within a few steps (you crossed a valley; the batch changed), so the trend must stay nimble. A parameter's typical gradient magnitude is a slow property of where it sits in the architecture ‚ÄĒ and since we divide by it, jitter there would inject noise into every step. Fast numerator, slow denominator. These defaults are so serviceable that most practitioners never touch them (LLM pretraining sometimes drops ő≤‚āā to 0.95 ‚ÄĒ a shorter scale memory reacts faster when gradient scales shift mid-training).

Closing chapter 3's thread: why 0.3162, and how Adam fixes it

A fresh EMA lies small ‚ÄĒ watch it

Feed an EMA (ő≤=0.9) a constant stream of 1.0's. It should report "the average is 1.0." It doesn't ‚ÄĒ not at first:

output ‚ÄĒ real run t=1: ema of constant 1.0 = 0.1000 corrected: 1.0000 t=2: ema of constant 1.0 = 0.1900 corrected: 1.0000 t=3: ema of constant 1.0 = 0.2710 corrected: 1.0000

Why it lies: the EMA started at 0, and that phantom zero still holds 90% of the weight at t=1, 81% at t=2‚Ä¶ The fix is almost embarrassing: at step t, the real data has received exactly 1 ‚ąí ő≤ŠĶó of the total weight (t=1: 0.1; t=2: 0.19; t=3: 0.271 ‚ÄĒ the very numbers in the left column!). So divide by (1 ‚ąí ő≤ŠĶó) and the lie cancels ‚ÄĒ right column, exact 1.0 every time. That division is all that mŐā and vŐā are.

Now the canonical first step, without and with the patch

Our old friends: w=2, gradient ‚ąí24, lr=0.1.

output ‚ÄĒ real run raw m=-2.400 v=0.5760 step WITHOUT correction = -0.3162 corrected m^=-24.0 v^=576.0 step WITH correction = -0.1000

Without correction: the small-lying m (‚ąí2.4) divided by the root of the small-lying v inflates to ‚ąí0.3162 ‚ÄĒ chapter 3's exact overshoot, inherited. With correction: mŐā = ‚ąí24 (the true gradient), vŐā = 576 = (‚ąí24)¬≤, so the step is 0.1 ¬∑ (‚ąí24)/‚ąö576 = 0.1 ¬∑ (‚ąí24)/24 = ‚ąí0.1000, exactly lr.

Pause on that last equality ‚ÄĒ it's the most quietly beautiful fact in this course: on its first step, with a fresh memory, Adam moves every parameter by exactly lr in its gradient's direction. Not 2.4 for the hot one and 0.0024 for the cold one ‚ÄĒ lr, for everyone, from step one. The wish from chapter 3 page 0, delivered to the digit.

The payoff, measured ‚ÄĒ and the honest caveat

Payoff 1: the 1000√ó scale spread, dissolved

output ‚ÄĒ real run: first-step size, lr=0.1 g= -24.000: SGD |step| = 2.4000 Adam |step| = 0.1000 g= -0.024: SGD |step| = 0.0024 Adam |step| = 0.1000

Chapter 3's wish-table, completed: hot and cold parameters both move exactly lr. This is the property that made Adam the default: lr means the same thing for every parameter ‚ÄĒ and, mostly, for every architecture, which is why lr folklore ("3e-4") transfers across models in a way SGD lr's never did.

Payoff 2: robustness to the lr knob ‚ÄĒ the bowl, swept

output ‚ÄĒ real run: steps to loss<0.01 on the bowl SGD : lr .005‚Üí459 .01‚Üí228 .05‚Üí44 .09‚Üí24 .11‚Üídiverges Adam: lr .1 ‚Üí213 .3 ‚Üí83 .5 ‚Üí54 1.0‚Üí49

SGD's step count swings from 459 to 24 to infinity across a 20√ó lr range. Adam's drifts from 213 to 49 across a 10√ó range ‚ÄĒ every setting works, none explodes. You can hand Adam a sloppy lr and get a usable run; that forgiveness is worth more in practice than any benchmark number.

The honest caveat ‚ÄĒ read the two tables again

Best SGD on the bowl: 24 steps. Best Adam: 49. On a clean, fixed landscape, well-tuned SGD(+momentum: 11!) beats Adam ‚ÄĒ normalizing away the gradient's magnitude also throws away real information about how far to go. Adam's wins are robustness and scale-spread, not raw speed on easy terrain. This is why convnet people with time to tune still swear by SGD+momentum, while everyone training transformers on a deadline uses Adam('s successor, next chapter). One real-network data point, our XOR net from the frameworks course:

output ‚ÄĒ real run: XOR MLP, steps to loss<0.01 sgd (lr .5) : 349 steps adam(lr .05): 34 steps

10√ó ‚ÄĒ because a real network, even a tiny one, has exactly the messy per-parameter scale spread Adam was built for (first-layer weights vs biases vs output layer), and nobody hand-tuned per-layer lrs.

Adam's bill: memory ‚ÄĒ counted on your fingers

Two extra floats per parameter, at LLM scale

m and v are each the same shape as the model. Derive the bill for a 7B-parameter model in fp32 (4 bytes per float):

params 7e9 √ó 4 B = 28 GB grads 7e9 √ó 4 B = 28 GB m 7e9 √ó 4 B = 28 GB ‚Ēź v 7e9 √ó 4 B = 28 GB ‚Ēė the Adam tax: 56 GB ‚ÄĒ HALF the training-state bill total ‚Čą 112 GB before a single activation is stored

The optimizer state outweighs the model. This single arithmetic fact explains a whole branch of systems engineering: mixed-precision recipes that keep m, v in special formats, 8-bit Adam (quantize the moments: 56 GB ‚Üí 14 GB), and ZeRO/FSDP sharding whose first target (stage 1) is precisely the optimizer state ‚ÄĒ you met FSDP in the frameworks course; now you know what it's sharding first and why. SGD+momentum, for comparison: one buffer, half the tax; plain SGD: zero.
