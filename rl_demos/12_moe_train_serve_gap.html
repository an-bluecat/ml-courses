Chapter 12: The MoE Training-vs-Serving Gap

The interview question: "GRPO on a Mixture-of-Experts model — what goes wrong between training and serving, and how do you fix it?"

First, Without MoE: Every RL Framework Runs TWO Copies of Your Policy

Why two? Because generating and training want opposite hardware tricks

Chapter 8's toy called generate() and loss.backward() on the same object. At scale nobody does that: generating a 20,000-token chain of thought token-by-token is brutally slow on a training stack, so rollouts run on a dedicated inference engine (vLLM or SGLang — paged KV cache, continuous batching, fused sampling kernels), while gradients run on a training engine (FSDP or Megatron — kernels optimized for big batched backward passes). Same checkpoint, two completely different programs.

Why the loss never notices — look at chapter 8's code again

old_lp = token_lp.detach() # computed by the TRAINER
new_lp = response_logprobs(model, ...) # computed by the TRAINER
ratio = torch.exp(new_lp - old_lp) # trainer / trainer

The probabilities of the engine that actually sampled the tokens — call it πinfer — appear nowhere. The importance ratio corrects staleness between two trainer snapshots, and silently assumes πinfer = πtrain. When that's false, you're doing off-policy RL with no correction: chapter 9's leak #3, the "secretly off-policy" problem (Yao, Liu et al. 2025 made this famous). Gradient bias with no alarm going off.

How big is the gap for a dense model? Tiny. Move on? Not yet.

Measured per-token KL between the two engines for a dense 8B model: about 6×10-4 — harmless-sounding, and mostly it is. Two things stop it from being harmless: (1) errors compound along autoregressive sequences (BF16's coarse rounding makes long sequences drift measurably — one study found switching both engines to FP16 shrinks the sequence-level mismatch ~24×), and (2) occasional single tokens go wild — one engine says p≈0.9, the other p≈0.01. Rare, but with 20k-token responses "rare per token" is "guaranteed per batch."

Still, dense GRPO mostly survives this. Now add a router.

MoE: A Rounding Error Picks a Different Brain

Recall what a Mixture-of-Experts layer does (systems course, ch. 4)

Instead of one big feed-forward block, the layer holds many "experts" (say 128 small FFNs). A router scores them per token and sends the token through only the top-k (say 8). The scores are ordinary logits; the selection is an argmax-style cutoff — and cutoffs are discontinuous: two candidates separated by 0.0003 are one rounding error away from swapping.

The flip, concretely

# router scores for one token, top-2 routing (illustrating the mechanism):
inference engine: scores = [2.3100, 1.4001, 1.3998] → top-2 = {expert 0, expert 1}
training engine : scores = [2.3100, 1.3999, 1.4002] → top-2 = {expert 0, expert 2}
# same weights. A ~1e-4 rounding difference — the SIZE of the normal
# dense-model wobble — just swapped which subnetwork processes this token.
# Expert 1 and expert 2 are entirely different sets of weights:
# the token's output, and every layer after it, now diverge for real.

In a dense model, a 10-4 wobble stays a 10-4 wobble. In an MoE, any wobble near a top-k boundary is amplified into executing different code. And with dozens of MoE layers × thousands of tokens, some router somewhere is always near a boundary.

Measured, not imagined (Qwen3-30B-A3B, SGLang vs Megatron-style trainer, same weights)

It's not only the two engines — the router churns between updates too

The Qwen team (GSPO paper) measured a second churn: after each single gradient update, roughly 10% of activated experts change for the same rollout sample — πθ vs πold inside the trainer. So chapter 8's ratio, which is supposed to measure "how much did my policy move," is heavily contaminated by "which experts happened to fire" noise. Router sensitivity poisons both comparisons GRPO relies on.

From Noisy Ratios to a Dead Run

The causal chain, step by step

1. router flips → token probs differ between sampler and trainer
2. the loss's ratio no longer measures policy change — it measures routing luck
3. a few tokens get extreme ratios → huge spurious gradient terms
(every token carries the full response advantage — ch. 8 p. 2 —
so one poisoned token weighs as much as a real learning signal)
4. updates chase noise; the noise compounds over steps
5. reward curve stalls, then collapses — often unrecoverably

Note where the clip fails you: clipping was designed for honest staleness (ratio drifting as θ moves). Ratio noise from routing flips sits inside the clip window most of the time — it doesn't get cut, it gets trained on.

What it looks like in practice (R3 paper's own baseline runs)

The Qwen team says it outright: vanilla GRPO on their MoE models would not converge at all without a routing-replay-style fix. Anyone who has actually run GRPO on a MoE has met this problem — which is what makes it a natural probing question for claimed GRPO experience. (It's one member of a whole family of documented production failure modes — see chapter 13, the failure-mode atlas.)

Why GRPO specifically gets hit hardest

  • Long reasoning rollouts (10k-30k tokens) — more tokens, more layers, more chances per sequence for a bad flip; sequence-level drift compounds.
  • Batch reuse / async rollouts — the honest staleness the clip must absorb is already large; routing noise rides on top.
  • Verifier rewards are sparse (one bit per response) — signal-to-noise is thin to begin with; poisoned ratio terms compete directly with the real gradient.

Three Fixes, Three Different Layers

Fix 1 — TIS: admit you're off-policy and correct for it

Truncated Importance Sampling (Yao, Liu et al. 2025). Record πinfer's per-token log-probs during rollout (the inference engine already computes them), then weight each token's gradient by the trainer/sampler ratio, capped:

weight_t = min( π_train(token_t) / π_infer(token_t), C ) # C = 2 typically

This is chapter 9's coin-flip correction, aimed at leak #3, with a cap for variance control. It fixes the gradient bias — but the two engines still run different subnetworks; you've reweighted the mismatch, not removed it. On MoE it delays the collapse (step 60 → 105 above) rather than preventing it.

Fix 2 — Routing Replay (R3): make both engines run the SAME subnetwork

This is the answer your interviewer was looking for: during rollout, record which experts each token used, at every MoE layer. During the training forward pass, don't let the trainer's router choose — replay the recorded choices.

# rollout (inference engine): for each token, each MoE layer,
# save the top-k mask alongside the generated tokens:
mask = top_k_experts(s_infer) # e.g. {expert 0, expert 1}
# training forward: SELECTION is replayed, WEIGHTS are recomputed:
gate_i = mask_i · exp(s_train_i) / Σ_j mask_j · exp(s_train_j)
# - both engines now execute the same experts → same subnetwork
# - gate weights still come from s_train → the ROUTER STILL GETS GRADIENTS
# (it learns how much to trust the replayed experts, and everything
# upstream of the router trains normally)

Effect: train-inference KL drops from 1.5×10-3 to 7.5×10-4 — right back at dense-model levels — and the extreme-token count falls by an order of magnitude. The run stops collapsing (table on page 2). Engineering note: the masks are cached with the KV-cache, so multi-turn/agentic rollouts replay them nearly for free.

Two names, one idea: the Qwen team had used an internal "Routing Replay" between πold and πθ; the R3 paper (PKU + Xiaomi, Oct 2025) formalized replaying inference-engine routing into training. Your friend's "把 rollout 时的 expert 记下来" is exactly this.

Fix 3 — GSPO: change the objective so it stops caring about single tokens

Qwen's shipped alternative: if token-level ratios are routing-noise garbage, stop using token-level ratios. Group Sequence Policy Optimization replaces them with one sequence-level ratio — the length-normalized product:

s_i = ( π_θ(response_i) / π_old(response_i) )^(1/|response_i|)
# = exp( mean of per-token log-ratios ) — individual flips average out
# clip range shrinks accordingly: ε ≈ 3e-4 instead of 0.2

One noisy token barely moves the mean of 10,000 log-ratios, so the objective is robust to routing churn — GSPO trains Qwen3's MoE models without replay. Trade-off: coarser credit (whole sequences, not tokens). And the R3 authors' head-to-head suggests alignment still helps: GSPO 66.76 < GRPO+R3 68.05 < GSPO+R3 69.00 in their multi-step setting.

Sidetrack: the root-cause school — just use FP16?

Sea AI Lab (arXiv 2510.26788) traces the whole mismatch to BF16's 7 mantissa bits: the two engines round differently and the error compounds along the sequence. Switching rollout + training to FP16 (10 mantissa bits) cuts sequence-level log-prob mismatch ~24× and stabilized their runs where BF16 collapsed. It attacks the wobble itself; R3 attacks the amplifier (the router); TIS attacks the gradient math. Layers of the same onion — and a good thing to mention unprompted in an interview.

Refresher: why not replay in serving instead — "record training experts and use them at inference"?

Direction matters. The tokens were generated by the inference engine's routing — those choices are frozen history; the trainer's job is to compute the probability of that history under the current weights. So training must replay inference's choices, not vice versa. At serving time there's nothing to replay against — the router just routes. (If you remember the fix as "记下训练时的 expert," flip it: record at rollout, replay at training.)

How this chapter connects

This is chapter 9's spectrum made physical: "on-policy" degraded not by design but by infrastructure — and the importance ratio (ch. 9 p. 2) reappearing as TIS. It's also chapter 8's warning label: the eleven loss lines are only as honest as the log-probs you feed them. If someone asks what makes production RL hard, this — not the math — is the answer.
