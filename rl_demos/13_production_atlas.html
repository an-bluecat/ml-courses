Chapter 13: The Production Failure-Mode Atlas

Chapters 10 and 12 each went deep on one way GRPO breaks at scale. This chapter is the map of the whole family — every documented member, its fix, and its sources.

Why "a Family" Is the Right Word

The claim, and the evidence for it

The MoE serving gap (ch. 12) and dead groups (ch. 10) are not isolated curiosities. They belong to a family of ~10 documented production failure modes that recur across three independent kinds of sources:

  • Frontier-lab tech reports — DeepSeek-R1, Qwen (GSPO), Kimi k1.5/K2, MiniMax-M1, Magistral (Mistral), Meta (LlamaRL, ScaleRL), ByteDance Seed (DAPO, VAPO) each hit and document several of them.
  • RL framework documentation — verl, TRL, NeMo-RL and slime ship the fixes as config options: TRL's GRPO trainer literally has loss_type, epsilon_high, scale_rewards knobs — this atlas encoded as a schema. verl has a "Rollout Correction" doc page treating several members as one problem class.
  • Recipe papers structured as fix-lists — DAPO is four of these fixes in one paper; Meta's ScaleRL (a 400k-GPU-hour ablation study, arXiv 2510.13786) systematically tests fixes for nearly every member.

When the same problem shows up in three labs' reports AND as a framework config flag, it's real, it's common, and it's fair game in an interview. That's the standard of evidence used for everything on the following pages.

One sentence that organizes all of them

Every member is a gap between what you think you're optimizing and what the machinery actually optimizes.

Family 1 — The Objective Quietly Weights Things Wrong

① Length bias — long wrong answers hurt less

# GRPO's original loss averages per sequence: each token's push = Â / |o|
wrong answer, 10 tokens : each token punished −1.0 / 10 = −0.100
wrong answer, 1000 tokens: each token punished −1.0 / 1000 = −0.001
# same total blame, 100x less pressure per token. The policy discovers it can
# "dilute" punishment by rambling → wrong answers grow longer → length explodes.

Symptom: mean response length climbs steadily — driven mostly by incorrect answers — with no accuracy gain. Fixes: token-level loss (normalize over the whole batch, not per sequence — DAPO); drop the 1/|o| entirely (Dr. GRPO); group-level length normalization (Magistral); explicit token budgets (Kimi K2).

Sources: Dr. GRPO arXiv:2503.20783 · DAPO 2503.14476 · Magistral 2506.10910 · TRL docs ship loss_type and call plain GRPO "not recommended due to length bias".

② Overlong truncation noise — punishing answers for being cut off

A response that hits the max-generation limit gets scored by the verifier as wrong — even if the reasoning was on track. The model gets punished for length, believes it was punished for content, and the signal turns to noise.

Fixes: mask truncated samples out of the loss ("overlong filtering") or apply a graduated penalty near the limit ("soft overlong punishment") — both DAPO; advantage-masking of truncated responses (Skywork-OR1); force an end-of-thinking phrase so a scoreable answer always exists (ScaleRL); or don't truncate at all — park the unfinished rollout and resume it next iteration (Kimi k1.5's partial rollouts, which resurface as problem ⑦'s fix).

Sources: DAPO 2503.14476 · Skywork-OR1 2505.22312 · ScaleRL 2510.13786 · Kimi k1.5 2501.12599.

③ The /std difficulty bias — chapter 10's problem, in its smooth form

# two groups, each with exactly ONE outlier (G=8, population std):
balanced [1,1,1,1,0,0,0,0]: std = 0.500 → advantages ±1.00
near-solved [1,1,1,1,1,1,1,0]: std = 0.331 → the lone failure gets −2.65
# dividing by a small std INFLATES advantages exactly on the prompts
# you least want to emphasize (nearly-solved / nearly-hopeless).
# std = 0 exactly → the dead group of chapter 10. Same disease, smooth version.

Fixes: drop the /std, keep mean-centering (Dr. GRPO); normalize advantages at the batch level instead of per group (REINFORCE++; ScaleRL converged on the same choice independently); quantile baselines (arXiv 2509.22611). TRL exposes this as the scale_rewards flag.

Sources: Dr. GRPO 2503.20783 · REINFORCE++ 2501.03262 · ScaleRL 2510.13786.

Family 2 — The Update Dynamics Kill Future Learning

④ Entropy collapse — the policy stops exploring, permanently

Symptom: policy entropy crashes within a few hundred steps; the G rollouts in a group become near-clones (which also multiplies ch. 10's dead groups); pass@k saturates and the run plateaus.

Mechanism, two documented layers:

# layer 1 — the covariance drain: tokens that are already high-probability AND
# high-advantage get reinforced hardest → entropy only ever flows downhill.
# (arXiv 2505.22617 derives performance = a - b·e^(entropy): you TRADE entropy for reward.)
# layer 2 — the clip is asymmetric against rare tokens. A reflection token:
p('wait') = 0.01 → 0.013 ratio = 1.30 > 1.2 → CLIPPED # exploration blocked
p('the') = 0.60 → 0.66 ratio = 1.10 < 1.2 → passes # the common token moves freely

MiniMax found exactly this killed their long-CoT training: "wait"/"recheck" tokens were clipped out after the first off-policy update. Fixes: Clip-Higher — raise only the upper bound, ε_high > ε_low (DAPO); restrict updates on high-covariance tokens (Clip-Cov/KL-Cov, with a shipped verl recipe); CISPO — clip the importance weight but never zero a token's gradient (MiniMax-M1, adopted by ScaleRL and NeMo-RL); adaptive entropy control (Skywork-OR1).

Sources: entropy mechanism 2505.22617 · DAPO 2503.14476 · MiniMax-M1 2506.13585 · Skywork-OR1 2505.22312 · five independent groups total.

⑤ KL-term pathologies — the leash strangles, or lies

Problem (a) — modeling: on long-horizon reasoning, the optimal policy must travel far from the initial model, so the KL penalty mostly fights progress. DAPO and Magistral both simply delete it for reasoning RL (ProRL keeps it but periodically resets the reference to the current policy — a ratcheting leash).

Problem (b) — estimation: the "k3" estimator you met in chapter 8 (exp(d) − d − 1) has an unbiased value but its gradient is only correct exactly on-policy. Under the real off-policyness of pages 4's systems, the KL loss silently pushes in slightly wrong directions. Recent analyses ("value unbiasedness ≠ gradient correctness") recommend gradient-correct estimators if you keep the term at all.

Sources: Schulman's estimator note joschu.net/blog/kl-approx.html · DAPO drops KL 2503.14476 · Magistral 2506.10910 · ProRL 2505.24864 · estimator analyses 2510.01555, 2512.21852.

Refresher: didn't chapter 8 say the KL term protects against gibberish?

Yes — and both things are true. The KL leash prevents degenerate drift when the target behavior is close to the reference (alignment-style tuning). For from-base reasoning RL the destination is legitimately far away, so labs remove the leash and rely on other guards (clipping, verifiable rewards, curriculum). Knowing when the same term helps vs hurts is precisely the kind of judgment interviews probe.

Family 3 — The Reward Isn't Measuring What You Think

⑥ Reward hacking — even "verifiable" rewards are proxies

Chapter 7 called binary verifiers "impossible to game." At production scale that needs asterisks — the optimizer reliably finds every gap between verifier passes and task solved:

exploitexampledocumented defense
weak equality checkers / parser bugsanswers formatted to fool the extractor; gaming symbolic-equality edge casesfuzz the verifier before training (arXiv 2606.01066); perturbation-test it (2604.15149)
neural reward modelsthe classic hack: outputs that score high but aren't betterDeepSeek-R1 avoided neural RMs entirely for this stated reason
rubric/judge rewards for open-ended taskssycophancy tics, praise-paddingKimi K2's anti-hack rubrics ("no initial praise…") + gating the critic on verifiable-task competence
objective under-specificationR1-Zero drifting into mixed-language reasoning (reward never said "stay in one language")add a language-consistency reward (DeepSeek-R1)

The unsettling one: spurious rewards — your reward curve may not mean anything

# "Spurious Rewards" (arXiv 2506.10947), on Qwen2.5-Math + MATH-500:
RLVR with ground-truth rewards: +29.1 points
RLVR with RANDOM rewards : +21.4 points # !!

Random rewards recovered two-thirds of the gain on that model family — the clipped update was mostly amplifying behaviors the pretrained model already had, regardless of signal. (The effect did not transfer to other model families.) Two takeaways worth quoting: a rising reward curve doesn't prove your reward works, and an RLVR result shown on one model family is a claim about that family, not about the method. Sanity-check on a second base model.

Sources: 2506.10947 · DeepSeek-R1 2501.12948 · Kimi K2 2507.20534.

Family 4 — The Samples Aren't From the Policy You Assume

The chain that generates this whole family

⑧ long CoTs have heavy-tailed lengths → sync rollout waits for the slowest → GPUs idle
↓ so everyone goes asynchronous / pipelined for throughput…
⑦ …which makes every batch STALE (generated by weights from k versions ago)
↓ and even with zero staleness…
⑨ …kernels aren't batch-invariant: the sampler never exactly equals the trainer
↓ and if the model is MoE…
ch.12: router flips amplify the wobble into different subnetworks → collapse

verl's docs treat this whole chain as one object — "everything that makes π_rollout ≠ π_old" — handled by one toolkit (importance-sampling corrections, from ch. 9's exchange-rate idea).

⑧ Stragglers → ⑦ staleness: the throughput-correctness trade

Stragglers: a few 100k-token trajectories hold a synchronous batch hostage (the motivating problem stated by every async-RL paper: Kimi k1.5, AReaL, LlamaRL, Magistral, ROLL Flash). Fixes: partial rollouts — cap the per-iteration token budget, park unfinished generations, resume next iteration (Kimi k1.5); interruptible workers + dynamic batching (AReaL); fully-async generator/trainer separation (Magistral, LlamaRL).

But every one of those fixes creates staleness: trajectories now come from older weights, or are even spliced across policy versions (a partial rollout resumed after an update). Fixes for the fix: bound the staleness (ScaleRL pipelines with k=8 versions max); decouple behavior policy from proximal policy in the objective (AReaL's decoupled PPO); importance-correct explicitly (LlamaRL's AIPO — shown necessary for stability at 405B); CISPO's bounded IS weights (MiniMax-M1).

Sources: Kimi k1.5 2501.12599 · AReaL 2505.24298 · LlamaRL 2505.24034 · Magistral 2506.10910 · ScaleRL 2510.13786.

⑨ Nondeterminism — the deterministic limit of chapter 12

Thinking Machines ran the same prompt through Qwen3-8B at temperature 0, 1,000 times: 80 distinct outputs. Cause: inference kernels aren't batch-invariant — your token's logits depend on what else happened to be in the batch. Their stated RL consequence is chapter 12's thesis verbatim: numerics differences "implicitly turn on-policy RL into off-policy RL." In their RLVR experiment: no correction → reward collapse; importance weighting → stable; batch-invariant kernels → true on-policy, KL exactly 0 (at ~60% throughput cost). ScaleRL found the cheap half of this fix independently: computing logits in FP32 alone moved their downstream asymptote from 0.52 to 0.61.

Sources: Defeating Nondeterminism in LLM Inference (Thinking Machines) · ScaleRL 2510.13786 · NeMo-RL docs note FP differences flip MoE routing.

⑩ The frontier member: agentic / multi-turn credit assignment

One scalar reward over a 100-turn tool-use trajectory: which turn deserves it? Group-relative advantages assume "same prompt, comparable rollouts" — stochastic environments and flaky tools break that. Heavily published (a 2026 survey covers 47 methods), used at scale (Kimi K2's agentic stack), but — honest label — no settled canonical fix yet. In an interview, present it as the open end of the atlas, not as solved.

Sources: credit-assignment survey 2604.09459 · practitioner's guide 2510.01132 · Kimi K2 2507.20534.

How to use this chapter

  • Don't memorize ten problems. Internalize the four gap types (page 0) — once you have them, you can re-derive the members.
  • Go deepest on the two you own end to end: dead groups (ch. 10 — with your own notebook run) and the MoE gap (ch. 12).
  • Length bias is the cheapest third: its whole mechanism is four lines of arithmetic (page 1).
  • The unifying idea, if one sentence is needed: the algorithm is rarely the bug — the gap between assumed and actual optimization is. (Also exactly the fraud case study's conclusion.)
