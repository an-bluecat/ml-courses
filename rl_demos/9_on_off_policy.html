Chapter 9: On-Policy vs Off-Policy

One question decides everything: who generated the data you're training on?

One Question, Two Words

The definition (LLM post-training version)

ON-policy

The training tokens were sampled from the current version of the model being trained. The model learns from what it just did.

OFF-policy

The training tokens came from anything else — humans, another model, or an older snapshot of the same model. The model learns from someone else's behavior.

That's the whole definition. Everything else in this chapter is consequences.

Place every method you know on one axis

It's a spectrum, not a switch. "Slightly stale" (rejection sampling, PPO's inner epochs) sits in the middle — and even PPO/GRPO aren't at the far right, as page 3 shows.

Wait — "an older snapshot of the same model" counts as off-policy?

Yes, and this is the point people miss. You saw it in chapter 8, page 4: after one optimizer step, the weights have moved, so the batch you generated a moment ago was generated by a different policy. Data goes stale the instant you update. "Off-policy" isn't about whose model it is — it's about whether the generator and the learner are the same weights.

Sidetrack: where the terms come from (classic RL)

In textbook RL (Sutton & Barto), a method is on-policy if it improves the same policy that collects the experience (SARSA), and off-policy if it learns a target policy from data collected by a different behavior policy (Q-learning: behaves ε-greedily, learns the greedy policy; DQN's replay buffer is off-policy by construction — it replays years-old transitions). The LLM community uses the same words with the same meaning; the "behavior policy" is whoever wrote the training text.

Why Fresh Data Beats Better Data

The driving-lesson argument

Two ways to learn to drive:

Off-policy: watch 10,000 hours of a chauffeur's dashcam. The chauffeur never drifts out of lane — so the footage contains zero examples of recovering from a drift. The first time you drift, you're in a state the training data never visited. You improvise. Badly.

On-policy: drive yourself with an instructor beside you. Every mistake you actually make becomes a lesson about that exact mistake. The data distribution is, by construction, the distribution of situations you get into.

The LLM version: one early wrong token, then what?

# teacher data (off-policy SFT) only contains perfect solutions:
"23×47: 23×40=920, 23×7=161, 920+161=1081."
# at inference the student writes 23×40=820 (its own typical slip).
# every following token is now conditioned on a prefix containing "820" —
# a prefix that appears NOWHERE in the teacher data.
# the model is off the training manifold; errors compound token by token.

This is called compounding error (in imitation learning: the DAgger problem; in seq2seq: exposure bias). The longer the generation, the worse it bites — which is exactly why it matters for reasoning models writing 10,000-token chains of thought.

On-policy training dissolves it: the model trains on prefixes it produces, including the ones containing its own "820"s — and the gradient pushes those continuations toward recovery or avoidance.

You already proved this to yourself in chapter 7

Chapter 7, page 5 ("Why not just SFT?"): rejection sampling throws away wrong answers and SFTs on the good ones — and hits a ceiling, because model v2 never learns what v2's mistakes look like, and nothing ever pushes the wrong tokens down. GRPO's negative advantages are on-policy criticism: aimed at the mistakes the current model actually makes.

Sidetrack: measured evidence, if an interviewer pushes

This isn't just a story: Tajwar et al. 2024 ("Preference Fine-Tuning of LLMs Should Leverage Suboptimal, On-Policy Data") and DeepMind's online-vs-offline study (arXiv 2405.08448) both find on-policy/online variants beat offline ones at equal data budgets; the RLHF literature consistently reports online methods outperforming plain DPO. There's even a memorable negative result from Thinking Machines (chapter 11's source): SFT on a model's own temperature-1 samples still degrades its behavior over time — finite-batch gradient noise drifts the weights, the dataset silently becomes stale, and "training on your own old outputs" turns off-policy. Freshness is a property you must actively maintain, not a one-time label.

So why does off-policy training exist at all? Because it's cheap.

The Importance Ratio — Trading With Stale Data, Correctly

The problem in one line

Policy-gradient math says: the gradient is an average over samples from the current policy. If your samples came from an older policy, the average is simply wrong — unless you reweight. The reweighting factor is the importance ratio.

Feel it with coins first — no LLMs

You want the average payout of a biased coin T: P(heads)=0.8, P(tails)=0.2. Heads pays $10, tails pays $0. True answer: 0.8×10 = $8.

But your samples come from a fair coin B: P(heads)=0.5. Say B gives you 10 flips: 5 heads, 5 tails. Naive average: 5×$10/10 = $5. Wrong — that's B's answer, not T's.

# fix: weight each sample by ratio = P_target / P_behavior
heads: ratio = 0.8 / 0.5 = 1.6 # T produces heads MORE often than B did
tails: ratio = 0.2 / 0.5 = 0.4 # T produces tails LESS often than B did
estimate = (5 flips × 1.6 × $10 + 5 flips × 0.4 × $0) / 10
= 80 / 10 = $8 # exactly right, from the "wrong" coin's data

That's importance sampling: count each sample as many times as the target policy would have produced it, relative to who actually produced it.

Same trick, per token — you've been using it since chapter 5

ratio_t = π_θ(token_t | prefix) / π_old(token_t | prefix)
# chapter 8's loss line: unclipped = ratio * advantage
# that ratio IS the coin correction: it converts "samples from π_old"
# into a valid gradient for π_θ. PPO/GRPO are importance-corrected
# policy gradients — that's the exchange rate that buys them the right
# to reuse each rollout batch for several updates.

Why not correct your way through ANY staleness, then? Variance.

The correction is exact on average but noisy sample by sample. The further apart the two policies, the wilder the ratios:

# policies close: ratios like 0.9, 1.1, 0.95 — calm, low noise
# policies far: ratios like 0.001, 0.02, 47.0 — one sample with ratio 47
# dominates the whole batch; the gradient is garbage

And per-token ratios multiply across a sequence — a 1,000-token response with slightly-off tokens compounds to an astronomically off sequence ratio. Hence every practical guard you've met or will meet:

  • PPO/GRPO clip (ch. 5, 8): cap the ratio at [0.8, 1.2] — refuse trades at extreme exchange rates.
  • Fresh batches: throw data away after a few epochs rather than correct forever.
  • TIS & friends (ch. 12): truncate ratios when even your "on-policy" data turns out slightly off-policy.
Refresher: is the clip "correct" math? No — it's a deliberate bias.

Truncating ratios biases the estimate (you're under-counting samples the target policy likes a lot). PPO accepts a small bias to kill a huge variance — a classic bias-variance trade. That's why the honest description of PPO/GRPO is "approximately on-policy with a variance-controlled correction," not "exactly on-policy."

The Fine Print: "On-Policy" Methods Mostly Aren't, Quite

Three leaks, in increasing order of surprise

#leakwho generated the data?the guard
1inner epochs — reusing a rollout batch for several optimizer stepsthe weights from a few steps agoratio + clip (ch. 8 p. 4: by pass 5, half the tokens were frozen out)
2async rollouts — at scale, generation GPUs and training GPUs run concurrently; rollouts arrive a version or two latethe weights from a few batches agosame ratio machinery, larger corrections; some systems cap staleness
3engine mismatch — the inference engine (vLLM/SGLang) and the trainer compute different probabilities for the same weights: different kernels, different roundinga policy that exists nowhere — π_vllm ≠ π_train even at identical weightsthis one is chapter 12's whole story (TIS, and for MoE: routing replay)

Leak 3 is the sleeper: your GRPO run can be "secretly off-policy" with no correction term at all, because the ratio in the loss uses π_θ/π_old — both computed by the trainer — and the actual sampler π_vllm never appears in the math. For dense models the gap is tiny; for MoE it can kill the run.

The one-sentence summary to carry into every later chapter

On-policy is a spectrum, not a bit.

SFT and DPO sit at the far off-policy end; PPO, GRPO and on-policy distillation sit near — but not at — the on-policy end; the importance ratio is the exchange rate between positions, and it gets noisier the further you stretch it.
