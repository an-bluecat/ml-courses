Transformer Ch3: Positional Encoding

Attention is a bag of tokens — order has to be smuggled in. This chapter designs the smuggling from scratch. All numbers are from real runs.

The problem, proven with a real run: attention cannot see order

The score between two tokens never mentions their positions

An attention score is q_i · k_j — a dot product of two vectors that were computed from the token embeddings alone. The subscripts i and j are only labels for us; nothing in the arithmetic uses them. So swap the tokens around, and every score survives the move. Don't take that on faith — run it:

emb = {"dog": torch.randn(8), "bites": torch.randn(8), "man": torch.randn(8)} Wq, Wk = torch.randn(8, 8) / 8**0.5, torch.randn(8, 8) / 8**0.5 def attn_weights(tokens): X = torch.stack([emb[t] for t in tokens]) Q, K = X @ Wq, X @ Wk return torch.softmax(Q @ K.T / 8**0.5, dim=-1) print("dog bites man:"); print(attn_weights(["dog", "bites", "man"]).round(decimals=3)) print("man bites dog:"); print(attn_weights(["man", "bites", "dog"]).round(decimals=3))
output — real run (torch.manual_seed(0)) dog bites man: tensor([[0.8730, 0.0490, 0.0780], [0.2970, 0.5190, 0.1840], [0.2780, 0.4530, 0.2680]]) man bites dog: tensor([[0.2680, 0.4530, 0.2780], [0.1840, 0.5190, 0.2970], [0.0780, 0.0490, 0.8730]])

Look closely: the second matrix is the first one flipped top-to-bottom and left-to-right — the same nine numbers, shuffled exactly the way the tokens were. "dog"'s attention row is [0.873, 0.049, 0.078] in both sentences; it just moved seats. No number anywhere records that the sentences mean opposite things. Whatever the rest of the network computes from these weights, it computes identically for "dog bites man" and "man bites dog".

The fix, in one line — the rest of the chapter is designing PE well

x_i = embedding(token_i) + PE(i) # stamp each token with its seat number

Now the vector for "dog" at position 0 differs from the vector for "dog" at position 2 — so its q and k differ, so the scores differ, so "dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog". The entire design question becomes: what should the stamp PE(i) look like? A good answer must satisfy two customers at once:

RequirementWhy
every position gets a distinct, bounded vectordistinct or it's no stamp at all; bounded or the stamp shouts louder than the word
"k seats apart" should look the same everywherethe pattern "adjective right before noun" should transfer from positions 3–4 to positions 703–704
Refresher: wait — doesn't the causal mask already tell the model about order?

Sharp question, and the honest answer is: partially, yes. In a decoder, token 7 can see tokens 0–6 but token 2 can only see 0–1 — that asymmetry does leak position information, and research models trained with no positional encoding at all ("NoPE") do learn to use it. But the leak is indirect and weak: it says "how many tokens are behind me", not "who is exactly 1 seat to my left". In practice every production model adds an explicit mechanism — and an encoder (bidirectional, no mask) has no leak at all: without PE, BERT literally could not tell subject from object. This chapter's demo is unmasked attention, where the invariance is total.

Design the stamp yourself — three failures, then the formula invents itself

Attempt 1: just use the number. PE(i) = [i]

Position 3 gets 3, position 500 gets 500. Distinct ✓. But the embedding values live around ±1, and this stamp grows without bound: by position 500 you are adding 500 to one coordinate of a word vector. The stamp shouts the word away. Fails "bounded".

Attempt 2: normalize it. PE(i) = [i / N]

Now it's in [0, 1] — bounded ✓. But N is the sequence length, so the stamp's meaning depends on how long the sentence happens to be: 0.5 means "position 5" in a 10-token text and "position 500" in a 1000-token one. "Two seats apart" is a different increment in every document. Fails "same everywhere".

Attempt 3: write i in binary — suddenly it's multi-scale

Bits are bounded (0 or 1), and a number's bits are unique. Look at what the columns do:

This is genuinely close: bounded ✓, unique ✓, and multi-scale — the fast bit pins your exact neighbor, the slow bit says which half of the text you're in. Two flaws remain: the values jump discretely (0→1 cliffs between adjacent positions, unfriendly to a network that likes smooth inputs), and there's no clean way to express "shift by k" as a simple operation on the bits.

Attempt 4 = the answer: replace each bit with a smooth wave

Keep the multi-speed ruler idea, but make each ruler continuous: a sinusoid. Fast waves for fine position, slow waves for coarse position — here are three real dimensions of a d=64 encoding, computed and plotted:

A position's encoding is a vertical slice through all the waves at once — a unique combination of phases. This is the published formula, verbatim, with the "bit index" i now choosing the wave's speed:

PE(pos, 2i) = sin( pos / 10000^(2i/d_model) ) PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos( pos / 10000^(2i/d_model) ) # each speed gets a sin AND a cos — page 2 shows why

Concretely, at d_model=4 there are two speeds: i=0 divides pos by 10000⁰=1 (fast), i=1 divides by 10000^½=100 (slow):

output — real run: the full encoding for positions 0–4, d_model=4 PE(0) = [ 0. 1. 0. 1. ] PE(1) = [ 0.841 0.54 0.01 1. ] <- [sin(1), cos(1), sin(0.01), cos(0.01)] PE(2) = [ 0.909 -0.416 0.02 1. ] PE(3) = [ 0.141 -0.99 0.03 0.999] PE(4) = [-0.757 -0.654 0.04 0.999]

Every requirement checked: bounded (everything in [−1, 1]) ✓, unique per position ✓, multi-scale ✓, smooth ✓ — and one property we haven't cashed in yet, which is the real reason for pairing sin with cos.

Refresher: why 10000? Where does that number come from?

It sets the range of the ruler set. The fastest wave (i=0) has period 2π ≈ 6 tokens; the slowest has period 10000·2π ≈ 63,000 tokens. Between them, wave speeds are spaced geometrically — like ruler markings at 1mm, 1cm, 10cm, 1m. The 10000 just guarantees that even the slowest wave hasn't repeated within any realistic training context (512–2048 tokens in 2017), so slices stay unique. It's a safety margin, not magic — RoPE keeps the same base, and long-context work (YaRN etc.) retunes exactly this knob.

The whole encoding at a glance — then verify its two famous properties

The PE matrix itself (real values, 32 positions × 48 dims)

Rows are positions, columns are dimensions, color is the value (blue −1 … orange +1). This is the picture the formula draws:

Read it left to right: fast zebra stripes → slower bands → near-constant. Read it top to bottom at any column: one wave, sampled. Each row is one position's stamp. No two rows match — the fast columns alone would need ~6 positions to repeat, and by then the slower columns have moved.

Famous property 1 — "shift by k" is one fixed matrix multiply, verified

Why sin AND cos per speed? Because a (sin, cos) pair is a point on a circle, and moving k positions ahead rotates that point by a fixed angle — the same angle no matter where you started. That's the trig identity sin(p+k) = sin·cos k + cos·sin k, packaged as a rotation matrix. Claim: one fixed matrix M₃ turns ANY PE(pos) into PE(pos+3). Verify it — build M₃ from k=3 alone (block-diagonal 2×2 rotations, one per speed), and hit PE(5):

output — real run, d_model=4 M_3 @ PE(5) = [ 0.9894 -0.1455 0.0799 0.9968] PE(8) = [ 0.9894 -0.1455 0.0799 0.9968] <- identical, to every printed digit

M₃ never saw the number 5 — only the offset 3. The same M₃ maps PE(0)→PE(3), PE(700)→PE(703). "Three seats to the right" is one linear operation, everywhere in the sequence. And attention heads are made of linear operations: W_Q and W_K can bake such rotations in, which is what lets a head learn "attend to the token 1 seat back" as a single reusable rule.

Famous property 2 — nearby stamps are more similar. True, with a catch

Dot the stamp of position 0 against every later stamp (d_model=128, real data):

output — real run, d_model=128, selected points off the curve PE(0)@PE(p) for p = 0, 1, 2, 3, 6, 10, 20, 40: [64. 62.1 57.4 52.2 47.1 42.8 38.9 32.6]

So a query with no opinion about content still gets a built-in recency lens: positions near it look more similar than positions far away. This is a soft, free prior — the model sharpens or ignores it through W_Q, W_K.

The catch: at tiny d_model this property is FALSE — check it

At d_model=4 (our worked example!), the real numbers refuse to decay:

output — real run, d_model=4 PE(0)@PE(p) for p=0..7: [2. 1.54 0.584 0.01 0.346 1.282 1.958 1.751]

Position 6 is more similar to position 0 than position 1 is (1.958 > 1.54)! With only two waves, the fast one swings the dot product around its full cycle. The smooth decay above is a law of averages over many frequencies — 64 waves at d=128 — not a property of any single sinusoid. Beware of d=4 intuition for d=4096 claims; this is why every plot on this page states its d.

The step everyone quietly worries about: x = embedding + PE

The worry, stated honestly

We take a vector that means "dog" and add a vector that means "position 7" — into the same coordinates. Doesn't the sum scramble both? If someone hands you 12 and says "that's a+b", you can't recover a and b. So how does the model un-mix them?

Answer 1: in high dimensions, the two ingredients barely touch

Two arbitrary directions in ℝ⁵¹² are almost always almost perpendicular. Check the actual overlap between a random embedding and a real PE vector:

e = rng.normal(0, 1, 512); e = e / np.linalg.norm(e) pe7 = PE[7] / np.linalg.norm(PE[7]) # d_model = 512 print("cos(random embedding, PE(7)) =", round(float(e @ pe7), 4))
output — real run cos(random embedding, PE(7)) = 0.0117

1% overlap. The sum e + PE(7) is like writing the word on the page and the seat number in the margin — same sheet of paper, different directions. A linear layer (W_Q!) can project onto "mostly word" or "mostly position" directions and read either ingredient nearly clean. And embeddings are trained knowing PE is there — they can organize to keep out of its way.

Answer 2: so why not concat and keep them perfectly separate?

You could — and it would work. But concatenating c position dims onto every token makes every weight matrix wider: d_model→d_model+c everywhere, paid in every layer forever. Adding costs zero parameters and zero width, and (see Answer 1) high dimension makes the sum approximately a concat anyway — the two signals live in nearly-disjoint subspaces of the same width. Bonus subtlety: addition lets position and content interact in the very first matmul, letting a head compute things like "noun AND early in sentence" in one shot.

The other classic recipe: skip the formula, learn the table (BERT, GPT-2/3)

pos_emb = nn.Embedding(max_len, d_model) # a trainable table: one row per position x = tok_emb(ids) + pos_emb(torch.arange(L)) # same ADD — the stamp is learned, not computed

Let gradient descent invent the stamps. Works exactly as well in-distribution — BERT and GPT-2/3 shipped this way. The cost is hard-coded reach: the table has max_len rows and row 2049 of a 2048-row table does not exist — no extrapolation, not even bad extrapolation. Sinusoidal at least produces a vector for unseen positions (just one the model never trained on). Neither degrades gracefully — which is the cliffhanger this chapter ends on.

Refresher: position 0 gets the same stamp in every sequence — is that a problem?

No — it's the point. PE(i) depends only on the seat, never the sentence, so "seat 3" contributes the identical vector to every training example. That consistency is what lets the model learn seat-dependent rules at all. All the sentence-specific information stays in the embeddings; PE is deliberately the boring, perfectly reliable part of the sum.

The modern move: don't stamp the token — put position inside the score

The pivot, in one sentence

Everything so far edits x_i before attention (an absolute stamp on the token). The score q·k then has to infer relative offsets from two absolute stamps. The modern methods skip the middleman: inject position directly into the score computation, in a form where only the offset i−j can survive.

RoPE: rotate q and k like clock hands — the score can only see the angle between them

Take q and k in 2D. Rotate q by (its position m) × θ, rotate k by (its position n) × θ. A dot product of two vectors depends only on their lengths and the angle between them — and that angle is (m−n)·θ. The absolute positions literally cancel in the geometry. Verify with real vectors — shift both positions by 100 and the score doesn't budge:

output — real run, d=4, random q and k R(q,5) @ R(k,2) = -0.963786 (positions 5 and 2 — gap 3) R(q,105) @ R(k,102) = -0.963786 (positions 105 and 102 — gap 3: IDENTICAL score) R(q,9) @ R(k,2) = -3.692908 (gap 7 — different gap, different score) plain q @ k = -3.386165 (no rotation at all)

That identical −0.963786 is the whole sales pitch: a pattern learned as "gap 3" at positions 5/2 fires unchanged at 105/102 — or 10005/10002. Full construction (pairs of dims as 2D planes, multi-speed θᵢ like the sinusoidal ruler set, the complex-number view) gets its own chapter: Ch3b.

ALiBi: don't encode position at all — just charge rent for distance

The bluntest instrument in the family: leave q and k alone, and subtract a penalty proportional to distance straight off the attention score:

score(i, j) = q_i · k_j / sqrt(d_k) − m · (i − j) # m: per-head slope, fixed
output — real run: the bias term alone, 4 tokens, slope m=0.5 (causal; nan = masked) [[-0. nan nan nan] [-0.5 -0. nan nan] [-1. -0.5 -0. nan] [-1.5 -1. -0.5 -0. ]]

Each query pays 0 to look at itself, m per step further back. Different heads get different slopes — steep heads become local specialists, shallow heads long-range. No stamps, no rotations, and distance-based penalties keep making sense at any length, which is why ALiBi extrapolates so well. The trade: it can only say "far = weaker", never "exactly 1 seat left" — less expressive than RoPE's full geometry.

Bottom line

In 2026, RoPE is the default in essentially every new open model, with context-extension methods (YaRN, LongRoPE) retuning its frequency base for 128K+ windows. Learned absolute survives in BERT-era encoders still in production. Sinusoidal is the ancestor you must understand — every later method is a reply to it.
